+ # Abstract

The Forkhead Box P2 (FOXP2) transcription factor is the first gene discovered to be specifically involved in the development of speech and language. Recent studies in birds, mice and fruit flies have shown that the gene exerts this involvement via its conserved function in a particular form of operant conditioning: operant self-learning. In operant self-learning, the subject learns about the outcomes of its own behavior and modifies the behavior accordingly. The neurobiology underlying this recently described form of learning is largely unknown, only Protein Kinase C and the FoxP gene family have been identified across taxa to be critically involved. The mechanism underlying operant self-learning has also been shown not to underlie other forms of learning, such as operant world-learning, allowing for a very rigorous behavioral analysis of the specificity of any of our neurobiological manipulations. In order to understand the mechanism by which FoxP mediates the neuronal modifications underlying operant self-learning, we need to find out which neurons express FoxP and which FoxP-dependent cellular processes underly memory formation. To this end, we will generate transgenic fly lines with which we will manipulate not only the expression of FoxP generally, but also the activity of FoxP expressing neurons. We will also generate monoclonal antibodies against the FoxP protein not only to study endogenous FoxP expression patterns, but also to validate and characterize our manipulations of FoxP expression. Behavioral experiments will then provide the information about which manipulation of FoxP expression in which neurons (or the activation/inactivation of which neurons) had a specific effect on operant self-learning (without affecting operant world-learning). The results of this research in the fruit fly will provide us with mechanistic insights into a novel form of behavioral learning (operant self-learning) which is one of the essential neurobiological underpinnings of speech and language acquisition in humans. As this form of learning is associated not only with speech and language disorders, but also with psychiatric disorders such as substance abuse, Tourette's or compulsive disorders, as well as with dyspraxias more broadly, the basic science on operant self-learning is instructive for clinical research in these areas as well. In fact, one instance of operant self-learning is already being researched as a treatment option for stroke patients, with the aim to increase their mobility.

+ One of the main mechanisms by which animals explore the world around them and learn how to choose adaptive behavior is "learning-by-doing" or operant learning. In operant conditioning experiments, at least two simultaneous components can be discerned: learning about causal relations in the environment (world-learning) and learning about the outcomes of one's own behavior (self-learning) [@10.1371/journal.pone.0100648 @10.1016/j.beproc.2011.02.005 @10.4161/cib.3.2.10334 @10.1016/j.cub.2009.06.014 @10.1016/j.cub.2008.07.041]. For instance, a rat pressing a lever for food can learn that when the lever is pressed (whatever the actual movement to press the lever), food will drop into the hopper, and/or the rat can learn that it needs to perform a series of movements to obtain food.

World-learning is mediated by the same, evolutionary conserved biochemical processes underlying synaptic plasticity, whether it is brought about by operant or by classical conditioning [@10.1016/j.cub.2008.07.041]. In world-learning, the sites of plasticity are commonly found along the sensory pathway (e.g., [@10.1101/lm.65303 @10.1126/science.7455692 @10.1101/lm.5.1.146 @10.1016/0304-3940(95)11309-K @10.1037/a0033979 @10.1523/JNEUROSCI.3286-12.2012 @10.3109/01677068509100140 @10.1016/j.neuron.2015.11.003]). Conversely, sites of plasticity have been identified along the motor control pathways in self-learning experiments, such as the cerebellum [@10.1126/science.1207403], the basal ganglia [@10.1073/pnas.1414542111 @10.1371/journal.pbio.0050321], central pattern generators [@Nargeot1999 @10.1126/science.1069434 @10.1016/j.neuron.2008.07.019 @10.1016/j.cub.2014.03.004 @10.1016/j.cub.2009.05.030] or motor neurons [@Wolpaw1994 @Wolpaw1995]. Unlike in world-learning, however, only two components of the biochemical pathways mediating neuronal change have been identified as evolutionary conserved in studies in humans [@10.1038/35097076] mice [@10.1126/science.1207403 @10.1073/pnas.1414542111], songbirds [@10.1111/j.1460-9568.2006.04763.x @10.1371/journal.pbio.0050321], snails [@10.1016/j.neuron.2008.07.019] and flies [@10.1016/j.cub.2008.07.041 @10.1371/journal.pone.0100648]: Protein Kinase C (PKC) and the transcription factor family FoxP.

There are two main areas of research required for a mechanistic understanding of self-learning. Which biochemical processes need to take place in which neural circuits for operant self-learning to occur? In the fruit fly *Drosophila*, both areas can be studied and this proposal addresses specific aspects of both questions.

Crucial for the study of self-learning is the experimental separation of the two components. In brief, operant self-learning experiments differ from composite experiments (i.e., where both world- and self-learning might occur) in the absence of a predictive external stimulus. As such, a composite experiment provides all the general motor and sensory controls as well as a control for general learning ability, while the self-learning procedure isolates the self-learning component from all other abilities shared by both tasks. This is accomplished by allowing stationary flying flies to use their yaw torque to control a punishing heat beam. In self-learning, no other stimuli are present during the experiment, forcing the fly to rely solely on its own behavior for predicting the occurrence of the heat. In the composite situation, an additional color stimulus is made contingent on the heat (and hence the yaw torque), allowing the fly to rely on both its own behavior and the color to predict the heat. In the latter situation, flies preferentially rely on and learn the color cue, rather than their own behavior. Hence, in the initial phases of this composite situation, operant world-learning appears to occur exclusively. Only with extended training is the self-learning component thought to start being engaged (i.e., habit formation starts; [@10.1371/journal.pone.0100648 @10.1016/j.beproc.2011.02.005 @10.4161/cib.3.2.10334 @10.1016/j.cub.2009.06.014 @10.1016/j.cub.2008.07.041]).
